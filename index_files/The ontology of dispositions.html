The ontology of dispositions

Updated Script

The motivation for analyzing the ontology of dispositions is to understand partial causes and objective chance. Objective chance is the primary interest here, motivated by a desire to understand the behavior of basic entities at the inorganic ontic level—specifically, entities that fall within the remit of quantum physics.

Dispositional properties, as described by Anjum and Mumford [1], will be used to clarify how properties of quantum entities can cause quantifiable property values to appear and influence other objects. This will develop into a dispositional understanding of probabilities in quantum mechanics.

While it is easy to appreciate dispositional properties intuitively, it can be harder to make the concept precise enough for a scientific theory. To assist, the following definitions are used:

Definition 1: A disposition is a property (such as solubility, fragility, or elasticity) whose actualization entails that the thing which has the property would change, or bring about some change, under certain conditions. Definition 2: A dispositional power is a physically existing dispositional property of an entity.

In everyday use, to say an object is soluble is to say it would dissolve if placed in liquid. Fragility implies it would break if dropped; elasticity implies it would stretch when pulled. The fragility is the disposition; the breaking is the actualization.

However, position and momentum are not “classical” dispositional properties. Results such as those of Kochen and Specker [2] show that the quantum situation is more subtle than solubility because they demonstrate a contradiction in assuming it is possible to assign simultaneous values to all observable properties in all states. Their result indicates the need for an ontology that deals with this context-dependent reality.

There is ongoing debate regarding the nature of dispositions [3], but the application in physics makes the ontology of causal powers the most attractive interpretation [4]. These powers exist independently of an observer.

With the goal of providing an objective interpretation of chance, Karl Popper [5, 6] introduced the propensity interpretation of probability:

“…waves (even those of the second quantization) are mathematical representations of propensities, or of dispositional properties, of the physical situation (such as the experimental set-up), interpretable as propensities of the particles to take up certain states.”

Popper proposed that the wave aspect of quantum mechanics determines the tendency of particles to assume specific states, with the strength of this disposition given by a probability. However, Popper developed his thinking prior to the full demonstration of non-contextuality, and he remained committed to a physics where particles have classical-like values that theory only describes statistically.

It is proposed here that properties of a physical entity need not all take values simultaneously to be physically existing. Dispositional properties govern how an entity tends to appear in various contexts. For example, a measurement apparatus may couple to an electron’s spin in a specific direction and instantiate a value; that is how the electron spin appears to that system.

In this dispositional theory, there are objective causal powers and the probabilistic appearance of property values. While dispositional properties cannot themselves appear in a Field of Sense, their effects can. This modifies the Field of Sense ontology (contrary to Markus Gabriel [7]) by recognizing that some properties affect appearances without appearing themselves.

For example, a porcelain cup exists and appears in a domestic Field of Sense even if it does not break. It has non-dispositional properties, like mass, that “pin it down” in a context. Similarly, an electron has intrinsic properties (charge and rest mass) that define its existence in the physical universe, within which it possesses dispositional spin, position, and momentum.

The Ontological Position on Dispositions:

  1. Physical objects have at least one property that appears or causes a value to appear in some Field of Sense.
  2. Dispositional powers cause property values to appear in Fields of Sense.
  3. Physical dispositions are properties of some physical objects.
